Richard Feldman (Jan 06 2024 at 19:13):

for example, we've talked before about demand for LAPACK bindings, which is ~1.5K linear algebra functions implemented in about 600K LoC of FORTRAN plus inline assembly

Richard Feldman (Jan 06 2024 at 19:18):

so here is a sequence of events that could plausibly happen if we add this feature:

  • someone makes a platform that does LAPACK bindings
  • someone wants to build a library on top of it, so they request that someone use module params to make a general platform-agnostic "LAPACK bindings" interface, which individual platforms can implement. It has 1.5k functions in it, because that's how big LAPACK is.
  • now various platform authors are pressured into implementing these bindings because that's the only way people can access it
  • also, there are different versions of LAPACK, some people for their use case want BLAS instead of LAPACK, or possibly both, and platform authors are now getting pressured to compile several hundred thousand lines of FORTRAN into their host binaries, even if they would only be used by a small percentage of the platform's users
  • a lot of platform authors say no to adding all that complexity for a small minority of use cases, so the people who really want those bindings say "fine, we'll fork the platform and add it ourselves" and now the ecosystem has basic-cli-lapack, basic-cli-lapack-v6.7, basic-cli-blas, basic-cli-blas-and-lapack, etc.
  • as a consequence of this situation, now there is demand for arbitrary C FFI outside platforms just so we can get out of the platform mess

Richard Feldman (Jan 06 2024 at 19:20):

so on the one hand, it would be cool to unlock those use cases

Richard Feldman (Jan 06 2024 at 19:21):

on the other hand, I don't like the idea of platforms forking just for the sake of exposing a different mix of non-I/O C FFI things

Richard Feldman (Jan 06 2024 at 19:23):

the nice thing about I/O is that it all boils down to a small, finite set of OS syscall wrappers, and everything else is a convenience wrapper around that - so I/O doesn't have that potential "ecosystem explosion" problem, and it also doesn't have the problem of side-effecting functions claiming to not be side-effecting
