Luke Boswell (Sep 19 2024 at 20:49):

From Claude

"Pure vs. Contextual" has the advantage of being relatively neutral while still conveying the key distinction: pure functions operate in isolation, while contextual functions interact with or depend on their environment in some way. This pairing avoids the potential misunderstandings of "effectful" or "stateful" while still being descriptive and forming a clear opposition.

If you prefer to stick closer to established terminology, using "Pure vs. Impure" but consistently explaining that impurity is not a negative quality, just a different set of tradeoffs (as Richard initially suggested), could help reshape the perception of the term over time.

Luke Boswell (Sep 19 2024 at 20:51):

ChatGPT

  1. Pure vs. Contextual:

    • Reasoning: This term emphasizes that the function interacts with some broader "context" (such as external state, time, or randomness). It avoids implying negativity or judgment and doesn't carry the specific connotations of state or side effects. It's neutral and broad, covering cases where the function is not pure due to context without necessarily being "effectful" in the strict sense.

  2. Pure vs. Stateful:

    • Reasoning: This highlights the function's interaction with state, whether internal (e.g., closures, mutable objects) or external (e.g., reading from external databases). It has intuitive clarity, though it may not fully capture non-deterministic behaviors like randomness that aren't state-based. Still, it draws a clear distinction between functions that rely on state and those that don't.

  3. Pure vs. Impure (with Reframing):

    • Reasoning: "Impure" is the traditional term and has clear oppositional meaning to "pure." The key here would be to reframe impurity as "just different," focusing on trade-offs rather than suggesting it’s a worse option. This leverages familiarity while reshaping perception.

  4. Pure vs. Effect-Possible:

    • Reasoning: This term emphasizes that the function could produce effects but doesn't imply it always does. It sidesteps the issue of implying an effect when it's only interacting with external state without causing side effects. It implies potential rather than certainty.

  5. Pure vs. Interacting:

    • Reasoning: This focuses on the fact that non-pure functions interact with something external to their inputs, whether that’s I/O, randomness, or mutable state. It captures a broad range of behaviors without being overly specific, while also framing the function in a neutral, descriptive way.

  6. Pure vs. Externalized:

    • Reasoning: This term suggests that the function’s behavior is influenced by something external, whether it’s reading external state or producing side effects. It clearly conveys that the function is not self-contained without necessarily implying judgment or specific mechanisms.
